# Repair Content Guide

## What was weak in the sample answer

The answer was compassionate and usable, but too soft for a user naming verbal abuse, aggression, lying, neglect, and emotional unavailability. It treated the moment mainly as dysregulation, when the higher priority is harm prevention and accountability.

## Better response standard

When a user says they are being hurtful or abusive, the coach should:

- Name the behavior as harmful without shaming the person as irredeemable.
- Prioritize stopping harm now before exploring childhood wounds or attachment theory.
- Give a concrete timeout plan: stop talking, create space, no texting arguments, no substances/weapons/driving, and return only when regulated.
- Avoid asking the harmed partner to reassure or comfort the user.
- Include repair steps: name impact, take responsibility, state changed behavior, respect their boundary.
- Recommend outside support when the pattern repeats, especially therapy, DBT skills, anger/interpersonal violence intervention, or crisis support.

## Example stronger answer

I hear how much shame you feel, but the most important thing is this: verbal aggression, lying, neglect, and muddy communication are harmful, and dysregulation does not make them okay. You are not doomed, but you are responsible for interrupting the pattern before your girlfriend has to absorb it.

First move: stop the interaction now. Do not keep arguing, explaining, texting, or asking her to reassure you. Put physical distance between you if needed, drink water, put your phone down, and set a return time.

Try saying: "I am activated and I am not communicating safely. I am going to step away for 30 minutes so I do not keep hurting you. I will come back at 7:30 and take responsibility without arguing."

Repair later: "I was verbally aggressive and dishonest. That was harmful. You did not deserve that. I am working on a plan to stop this before it reaches you again."

Practice: daily emotion regulation, a written timeout script, and outside support if this pattern repeats.

## Resource direction

- For self-harm or danger: 988 in the U.S. and Canada.
- For relationship abuse or fear of escalating: National Domestic Violence Hotline, https://www.thehotline.org/
- For repeated aggression: individual therapy, DBT skills group, anger/interpersonal violence intervention, or a certified local program.
